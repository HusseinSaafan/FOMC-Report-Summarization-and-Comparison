# FOMC Reports Brief

## January

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee decided to maintain the federal funds rate target range at 4-1/4 to 4-1/2 percent. Economic activity is expanding at a solid pace, with stable labor market conditions, though inflation remains elevated. The Committee assesses that risks to its employment and inflation goals are balanced. It is committed to achieving maximum employment and sustaining a 2 percent inflation rate. Ongoing reductions in Treasury and agency securities will continue.

## Why It Matters
This decision reflects a cautious approach amid economic uncertainties, focusing on data-driven assessments for future policy adjustments. The continued focus on balancing inflation and employment highlights the complexity of the current economic landscape.

_This is the first report in the series — no prior month to compare against._

## March

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee maintained the target range for the federal funds rate at 4-1/4 to 4-1/2 percent, noting resilient economic activity and solid labor market conditions. While inflation persists at elevated levels, uncertainty around the economic outlook has increased. The Committee plans to slow its securities holdings decline effective April, reducing the Treasury securities redemption cap to $5 billion monthly. All members except Christopher J. Waller supported the policy action, with Waller favoring no change in the target range and maintaining the current pace of securities decline.

## Why It Matters
The statement underscores a cautious approach, balancing ongoing economic growth and inflation pressures. The adjusted securities redemption strategy and Waller's dissent highlight differing views on pacing in a variable economic landscape.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The current statement emphasizes increased uncertainty about the economic outlook compared to the previous one. Additionally, it specifies a reduction in the monthly redemption cap for Treasury securities from $25 billion to $5 billion starting in April. There was a dissent from Christopher J. Waller, who voted against the policy action, preferring no change to the target range and suggesting a continuation of the current pace of securities decline.

## Net Interpretation
The shifts reflect a more cautious stance, with the Committee acknowledging heightened uncertainty while adjusting the pace of securities holdings decline. The dissent points to internal divisions regarding policy direction.

## May

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee maintained the federal funds rate target range at 4.25% to 4.50%. Economic activity is expanding solidly, with the unemployment rate stable and labor market conditions still solid. Inflation, however, remains somewhat elevated, and uncertainty regarding the economic outlook has increased, presenting risks of higher unemployment and inflation. The Committee will continue its balance sheet operations while closely monitoring incoming data to inform future policy adjustments.

## Why It Matters
This statement highlights the Federal Reserve's commitment to data-driven decision-making amid rising uncertainty, indicating a cautious approach to potential policy changes. The emphasis on ongoing balance-sheet operations reveals a sustained focus on achieving its dual mandate of maximum employment and stable inflation.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The current statement introduces a more pronounced caution regarding the economic outlook, citing an increase in risks of higher unemployment and inflation, a shift from previous wording. The wording on balance-sheet operations remains consistent, but the previous reduction in the Treasury securities redemption cap is now absent. Furthermore, Christopher J. Waller no longer dissents; all voting members aligned with the monetary policy decision.

## Net Interpretation
The modifications suggest a more cautious stance amidst increasing economic uncertainty, highlighting potential risks without altering the policy rate or balance-sheet strategy significantly.

## June

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee decided to maintain the target range for the federal funds rate at 4-1/4 to 4-1/2 percent, citing solid economic expansion and low unemployment, while acknowledging somewhat elevated inflation. Risks to the economic outlook remain notable, but uncertainty has diminished. The Committee reiterated its commitment to achieving maximum employment and a 2 percent inflation rate over the long run. It will continue to reduce its holdings of Treasury and agency securities and assess incoming data for future adjustments.

## Why It Matters
This decision underscores the Federal Reserve's cautious approach, balancing current economic strength with ongoing inflation concerns. The emphasis on data dependence highlights the need for vigilance regarding future economic developments.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The current statement indicates a diminished but still elevated uncertainty regarding the economic outlook, contrasting with the previous emphasis on increasing uncertainty. Additionally, the labor market commentary has shifted to emphasize that the unemployment rate "remains low," rather than "has stabilized at a low level." There were no dissents noted in either statement.

## Net Interpretation
The adjustments reflect a slightly more optimistic tone regarding uncertainty, while maintaining a watchful stance on employment and inflation. Overall, the communication remains neutral with a cautious outlook.

## July

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee decided to maintain the target range for the federal funds rate at 4¼ to 4½ percent, acknowledging moderated economic activity and solid labor market conditions. Inflation remains somewhat elevated, and uncertainty about the economic outlook is high. Dissent was noted from Michelle W. Bowman and Christopher J. Waller, who preferred a ¼ percentage point reduction in the target range. The Committee will continue to reduce its holdings of Treasury securities and agency debt.

## Why It Matters
The cautious stance reflects ongoing assessments of incoming data regarding inflation and employment, indicating potential adjustments to policy if necessary. The focus on gradual changes may signal a careful navigation of economic uncertainties ahead.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The most notable change in the current statement is the acknowledgment of moderated economic activity, compared to the previous solid growth assessment. Additionally, there was a dissent from Michelle W. Bowman and Christopher J. Waller, who preferred a ¼ percentage point reduction in the target range for the federal funds rate, marking a shift from the previous unanimous vote. The reference to heightened uncertainty in the economic outlook has also been reiterated.

## Net Interpretation
The overall tone appears more cautious, reflecting concerns over economic moderation and dissent among members, suggesting a slightly more cautious approach amid ongoing inflation challenges.

## September

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) lowered the target range for the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point to 4 to 4-1/4 percent. Recent economic indicators indicate moderated growth, slower job gains, and elevated inflation. The Committee expressed heightened uncertainty regarding the economic outlook, noting increased downside risks to employment. Voting unanimously favored the rate change except for Stephen I. Miran, who preferred a 1/2 percentage point cut.

## Why It Matters
The FOMC's decision reflects a cautious adjustment to monetary policy amidst evolving economic conditions. The focus on data dependency and risks underscores the dual mandate regarding employment and inflation as the Committee navigates potential challenges ahead.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The current statement signals a 1/4 percentage point reduction in the federal funds rate target range to 4 to 4-1/4 percent, contrasting with the previous maintenance of 4¼ to 4½ percent. Additionally, labor market wording now indicates slowed job gains and highlights increasing downside risks to employment. A new dissent from Stephen I. Miran is recorded, preferring a 1/2 percentage point cut.

## Net Interpretation
The shifts are mildly dovish, reflecting a response to moderated growth and mounting employment risks. The Committee's data-dependent approach remains central as they adjust monetary policy in uncertain economic conditions.

## October

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee lowered the target range for the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point to 3.75 to 4 percent. Economic activity is expanding moderately, though job gains have slowed and unemployment has slightly increased. Inflation remains elevated, and uncertainty regarding the economic outlook has risen. The Committee concluded the reduction of its aggregate securities holdings on December 1. Voting against the action were Stephen I. Miran, who favored a larger rate cut, and Jeffrey R. Schmidt, who preferred no change.

## Why It Matters
The adjustments reflect a cautious approach to managing risks to employment and inflation. Market participants should monitor forthcoming data closely, as it will inform future policy decisions.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The FOMC lowered the federal funds rate target range by 1/4 percentage point to 3.75 to 4 percent, down from 4 to 4.25 percent. The statement notes an expansion in economic activity as moderate, contrasting with previous wording that indicated a moderation in growth. Additionally, the Committee decided to conclude the reduction of its aggregate securities holdings on December 1, a new detail absent from the prior statement.

## Net Interpretation
The overall tone appears slightly dovish, signaling a cautious approach toward monetary policy and a focus on evolving economic conditions amid elevated inflation and uncertainty.

## December

### Summary

## Snapshot
The Committee lowered the target range for the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point to 3.5% to 3.75%, noting moderate economic expansion and elevated inflation. Job gains have slowed, and the unemployment rate has modestly increased, with downside risks to employment noted. The Committee judges that reserve levels are ample and will initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasury securities as needed to sustain reserves. A split vote occurred, with three dissenting members advocating for different actions.

## Why It Matters
This decision underscores the Committee's commitment to balancing employment and inflation goals, while adapting policy in response to evolving economic conditions. Monitoring data will be crucial for guiding future adjustments to monetary policy.

### Comparison to Previous Month

## Key Deltas
The Committee lowered the target range for the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point to 3.5 to 3.75 percent, a more accommodative stance than previously set at 3.75 to 4 percent. Additionally, the current statement indicates a shift towards purchasing shorter-term Treasury securities to maintain an ample supply of reserves, contrasting with the previous conclusion of the reduction in aggregate securities holdings. Dissent increased with three members opposing the action, highlighting a broader disagreement than before.

## Net Interpretation
Overall, the shifts signal a more dovish stance, as indicated by the lower rate target and proactive reserve management, reflecting increased caution amid considerable economic uncertainty.
